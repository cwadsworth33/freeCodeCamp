Alexander Berkman

Prominent anarchist writer and activist

    Timeline

  • 1870 - Born in the city of Vilnius in the Russian Empire
  • 1881 - Tsar Alexander II is assasinated. Berkman's uncle, Mark Natanson, is exiled for revolutionary activities. Berkman, influenced by growing radical sentiment, grows interested in some radical philosophies of the time, such as populism and nihilism.
  • 1882 - He is influenced by the Nikolay Chernyshevsky novel, "What is to Be Done?" as a school student in Kovno, Lithuania. He joins a revolutionary literature group at school and is demoted for a year after distributing banned materials and espousing atheistic views.
  • 1887 - His mother dies and he is given to his other uncle, Nathan Natanson. Berkman is expelled from school after stealing school exams.
  • 1888 - Berkman travels with his brother to Germany, then decides to emigrate to the United States
  • 1888-1892 - In New York, he becomes involved in revolutionary activities and contributes to several anarchist newspapers. He meets Emma Goldman, a fellow anarchist who becomes a lifelong friend. He becomes involved with Johann Most, an advocate for propaganda of the deed (violent acts carried out to incite a response from the masses)
  • 1892 - He moves to Worcester, Massachusetts with Goldman and Modest Aronstam, his cousin, where they open a luncheonette. Berkman attempts and fails to assassinate Henry Clay Frick, an anti-union factory manager involved in the Homestead strikes in Pittsburgh.
  • 1892-1906 - Berkman stands trial and is sentanced to 21 years in the Western Penitentiary and 1 year at the Allegheny County Workhouse. His appeals fail and he attempts to escape, which also fails. He writes Prison Blossoms with two other prisoners while incarcerated.
  • 1906 - He is released after serving 14 years. He reuinites with Emma Goldman and they both work on the journal Mother Earth.
  • 1911 - Berkman helps to establish the Ferrer Center in New York, an alternative school (open to adults and children) based on Ferrer's Spanish school intended to educate from a non-traditional perspective. Emphasis was on students learning through interacting with the world, not staying behind a desk.
  • 1912 - Berkman releases Prison Memoirs of an Anarchist, an account of his time in prison and one of his most well-known works.
  • 1917 - The US enters World War I. Berkman and Goldman form the No Conscription League, an organization against the newly instated Selected Service Act, in NYC. They are arrested under the Espionage act of 1917 for trying to dissuade people from signing up for the draft.
  • 1919 - Berkman and Goldman are released from prison. They and several hundred other anarchists are deported to Russia under the Anarchist Exclusion Act (AKA Immigration Act) of 1918.
  • 1922 - Berkman and Goldman became disillusioned after the Russian revolution did not produce the freedom and prosperity they had hoped. Berkman published The Bolshevik Myth in 1925, an account of his experiences in Russia and the results of the October revolution.
  • 1929 - Berkman, now living in France, publishes Here And Now: An ABC of Communist Anarchism, an introduction to anarchist principles written in clear, plain language.
  • 1936 - Berkman, suffering from poor health, commits suicide weeks before the Spanish Revolution.

"If your object is to secure liberty, you must learn to do without authority and compulsion. If you intend to live in peace and harmony with your fellow-men, you and they should cultivate brotherhood and respect for each other. If you want to work together with them for your mutual benefit, you must practice cooperation. The social revolution means much more than the reorganization of conditions only: it means the establishment of new human values and social relationships, a changed attitude of man to man, as of one free and independent to his equal; it means a different spirit in individual and collective life, and that spirit cannot be born overnight. It is a spirit to be cultivated, to be nurtured and reared, as the most delicate flower is, for indeed it is the flower of a new and beautiful existence."

- Alexander Berkman on preparation for the revolution, Here and Now: An ABC of Communist Anarchism
